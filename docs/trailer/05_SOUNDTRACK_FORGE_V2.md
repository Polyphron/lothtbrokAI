## How Suno Works — The Three Fields

Suno has **three separate input fields**. Using them correctly is the difference between a song and a score.

### Field 1: Style of Music
This is where you paint the sonic world. Every instrument, every mood, every texture.
**Only positive descriptions.** Do not put "no" or "without" here — Suno ignores negation in this field.

### Field 2: Lyrics / Structure
For instrumental scores, this is your **timeline**. Section markers with duration in seconds or minutes.
The more lines you write, the longer the track. Suno counts lines, not musical intelligence.
**Pad with `....` lines** between sections — each line of dots is roughly 3-5 seconds of sustained music.

### Field 3: Negative Prompt
This is where ALL the "don't want" goes. Suno has a dedicated field for this.
**Never put negatives in Field 1 or 2.** They do nothing there. Everything unwanted goes here.

## The Sound We Want

We are scoring a **1980s–1990s orchestral film soundtrack**. The golden age. Before everything became digital walls of synthesizer.

**Our ancestors:**
- **Basil Poledouris** — *Conan the Barbarian* (1982). Anvil percussion. Massive brass. Choral thunder. The greatest sword-and-sorcery score ever recorded.
- **James Horner** — *Braveheart* (1995). Solo instruments crying over a distant orchestra. Loneliness made audible. A single voice against the weight of war.
- **Jerry Goldsmith** — *The 13th Warrior* (1999). Dark. Percussive. Ethnic instruments woven into orchestral fabric. Viking drums that sound like the earth is breaking.
- **Ennio Morricone** — *The Mission* (1986). Sacred choral over gut-string instruments. The sound of faith and loss in a world too vast for any one man.

**What this means in practice:**
- Real orchestral instruments, recorded warm and analog
- Solo instruments that carry melody (a single horn, a single bowed string)
- Massive percussion — war drums, frame drums, anvil hits, timpani
- Male choral — not angelic, not pretty — battle-chant, liturgical, monastic
- Reverb from physical space — stone cathedral, wooden hall — not digital reverb
- Everything sounds like it was recorded on tape in a cold room in 1988

## Duration — How to Make Long Tracks

Suno defaults to ~2 minutes. Here's how to push past 4:

1. **Time markers in seconds** — `[0:00–0:45 — Opening passage]` tells Suno how long to hold
2. **Padding lines** — every `....` line adds ~3-5 seconds of sustained music
3. **More sections** — 8-12 sections instead of 4-5
4. **Repeat and evolve** — bring themes back in variation, Suno treats reprises as new material
5. **Use Suno's Extend** — generate 4 minutes, then extend from the endpoint for 6+ minutes

> [!IMPORTANT]
> The `....` padding trick is essential. A section with 6 lines of `....` after the marker
> will sustain for ~20-30 seconds. Without padding, sections collapse to ~10 seconds.

## If It Still Sounds Wrong

| Problem | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Sounds like a song | Remove ALL text from Field 2 except `[Instrumental]` markers and `....` |
| Too modern / digital | Add `warm analog recording` to Field 1, add `synth, electronic, digital, modern` to Field 3 |
| Too short | Add more `....` padding lines and more sections |
| Synth pads appearing | Field 3: `synth pad, ambient pad, electronic pad` |
| Happy/bright tone | Field 3: `major key, upbeat, happy, cheerful, bright` |
| Wrong era sound | Field 1: specify `1990s cinematic` or `1980s orchestral` explicitly |
| Vocals appearing | Field 2: put `[Instrumental]` as the very first line |
